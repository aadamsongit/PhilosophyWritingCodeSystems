## Be Exhaustive in Approach

When you're trying to break into software engineering or move to a better role, don't rely on a single channel.

Apply directly. Talk to recruiters. Keep your LinkedIn updated. Maintain a source-of-truth resume. Build projects. Document your work. Reach out to former colleagues. Contact staffing agencies. Participate in technical communities. Practice interviews. Improve your GitHub. Write about what you're learning.

Not every channel will work equally well, and not every channel will work at the same time. The point is to avoid mistaking one failed path for the whole market. A thorough approach gives you more data. You learn which resumes get responses, which recruiters understand your background, which roles align with your experience, and which interview formats expose your current bottlenecks.

Being exhaustive doesn't mean being frantic. It means testing multiple credible paths, observing what happens, and refining your strategy based on evidence. The goal isn't to do everything forever. The goal is to discover which channels produce signal, then invest more intentionally in the ones that work.

## LinkedIn Is Still King for Professional Networking

LinkedIn is still one of the most important platforms for professional networking in tech.

It is where recruiters search, where hiring managers may check your background, where former colleagues can stay connected, and where your work history can become more discoverable. A strong LinkedIn profile does not replace a resume, portfolio, GitHub profile, or interview performance. But it can make all of those things easier to find and easier to understand.

Use LinkedIn to communicate your value clearly. Keep your headline, experience, skills, and featured projects aligned with the kinds of roles you want. Connect with former colleagues, recruiters, staffing agencies, and people in technical communities. LinkedIn works best when it reinforces the rest of your professional story rather than trying to carry the entire job search by itself.

## There's No Hidden Job Board

People often talk about the "hidden job market" as though there's a secret list of opportunities available only to insiders.

My experience has been different. There isn't so much a hidden job board as there are many small actions that create optionality over time. Keeping your LinkedIn current. Maintaining relationships with former coworkers. Building in public. Writing technical articles. Speaking with recruiters. Working with staffing agencies. Contributing to open source. Maintaining a portfolio. Doing good work that people remember.

None of these actions guarantees a job. Together, they increase the number of ways opportunities can reach you. Many engineering careers are shaped by a series of unexpected conversations, referrals, recruiter messages, consulting opportunities, and recommendations that would never have appeared on a traditional job board.

I've also seen opportunities emerge from places people don't normally think of as recruiting channels. Engineers have been hired after participating in small Discord communities, through Reddit discussions or direct messages, from open-source communities, and from relationships that began simply by helping someone solve a technical problem. The goal isn't to chase hidden opportunities. The goal is to build enough credibility, visibility, and professional relationships that opportunities have more paths to find you.
