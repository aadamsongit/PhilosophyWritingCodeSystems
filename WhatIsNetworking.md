# What Is Networking?

LinkedIn talks a lot about networking, but the term needs clarifying.

Networking is not simply collecting connections, sending cold messages, or attending events. Those can be part of it, but they are not the whole practice.

At its best, networking means building professional relationships over time. That can include former coworkers, recruiters, staffing agencies, hiring managers, open-source maintainers, Discord communities, Reddit discussions, technical writers, mentors, peers, and people you've helped along the way.

The goal is not to know everyone. The goal is to create enough trust, visibility, and shared context that opportunities have more paths to reach you. Good networking is cumulative. It comes from consistent, genuine interactions: doing good work, following up, sharing useful information, asking thoughtful questions, and being someone people remember positively.

## Networking Is Not a Replacement for Technical Skill

Networking is one way technical skill becomes visible.

You should not assume that strong relationships or a polished online presence will allow you to bypass technical evaluations. Many companies will still expect you to demonstrate your engineering ability through interviews, coding exercises, system design, debugging, code review, or work samples.

What networking can do is create opportunities that might not have existed otherwise. It can help your resume get seen, earn you a referral, start a conversation with a recruiter, or lead to an interview.

After that, your technical ability still has to carry the process.

One observation that has aged well is that networking tends to become more valuable as your skills and experience grow. The more engineering judgment, domain expertise, and professional accomplishments you accumulate, the more substance there is behind the relationships you've built.
