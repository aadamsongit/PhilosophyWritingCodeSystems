## Debugging

Some interviews begin with broken code and ask you to identify and fix the problem. The emphasis is less on writing code from scratch and more on understanding an unfamiliar system.

These interviews reward careful observation, hypothesis formation, and systematic investigation. Rather than asking, "How would you build this?", they ask, "Why isn't this working?"

Developers with backgrounds in testing, QA, production support, or troubleshooting often feel comfortable in this format because they're accustomed to isolating problems, reproducing bugs, and validating fixes.

A good debugger doesn't jump to conclusions. They gather evidence, eliminate possibilities, and narrow the search space until the root cause becomes clear. Like many forms of engineering, debugging is an exercise in problem decomposition.

A good way to practice for debugging challenges is to inspect real codebases. Check sources, DevTools. Experiment with UIs.

## Take-Home Projects

Take-home projects have long been one of the more debated interview formats.

For years, many developers viewed them negatively because they could require significant unpaid time and sometimes lacked clear evaluation criteria. More recently, as live coding interviews have become a common source of frustration, I've noticed more developers expressing appreciation for take-home projects by comparison.

One advantage of a take-home project is that it more closely resembles real software development. Candidates have time to think, research, experiment, and revise their work rather than solving problems under a strict time limit.

For some developers, including many with AuDHD or other neurodivergent working styles, that environment can make it easier to demonstrate their actual engineering abilities. The emphasis shifts from rapid recall toward thoughtful implementation and technical judgment.

That doesn't mean take-home projects are free of tradeoffs. They can still require substantial time, and expectations should be reasonable for the role being filled.

In my experience, the current hiring market seems to rely less on take-home projects than it once did. Many companies have shifted toward live coding sessions, conversational technical interviews, or AI-assisted feature implementation exercises instead.

Like every interview format, take-home projects measure some skills well and others less effectively. Understanding those tradeoffs is more useful than assuming any one format is universally better.

## Code Review

Code review interviews can feel deceptively informal.

Rather than asking you to write software from scratch, the interviewer presents an existing implementation and asks you to evaluate it. Your task is to identify bugs, question assumptions, suggest improvements, and discuss tradeoffs. These interviews measure engineering judgment more than typing speed.

A good review goes beyond style comments. Look for correctness, maintainability, readability, architecture, testing, performance, security, and consistency with the existing codebase. Don't be afraid to ask questions. If part of the implementation depends on missing context, say so. Good reviewers distinguish between confirmed problems and assumptions that require clarification.

This is also one of the interview formats where AI has changed expectations. Reviewing generated code has become an increasingly important engineering skill. Whether the implementation came from a teammate, an AI assistant, or your own past work, the responsibility is the same: understand it before approving it.

In many ways, a code review interview asks the same question as a debugging interview. Instead of asking, "Can you write this?" it asks, "Can you reason about code someone else has already written?"

## System Design

Candidates discuss how they would design an application or service. Depending on the level of the role, this may range from a small feature to a large distributed system.

These interviews emphasize decomposition, architecture, scalability, and tradeoff analysis.

## Technical Discussion

Some interviews are primarily conversational. You may be asked about projects you've built, technologies you've used, decisions you've made, or tradeoffs you've encountered.

It's likely a recruiter screen will contain some of this. Strong STAR stories help. It helps to have good stories from your projects and prior work and good explanations for gaps.

## Paid Work Trials

I've noticed paid work trials appearing more frequently in discussions about software engineering hiring.

Rather than asking candidates to complete a traditional coding challenge, some companies invite them to work on a real or representative task over a limited period of time while compensating them for their effort.

Many developers view this as a positive candidate experience. It avoids unpaid take-home assignments and allows both the candidate and the company to evaluate what it's actually like to work together.

Paid work trials also test a broader range of engineering skills. In addition to writing code, candidates may need to navigate an unfamiliar codebase, communicate with teammates, ask clarifying questions, review pull requests, prioritize work, and deliver incremental progress under realistic constraints.

For candidates with strong portfolios or substantial professional experience, some companies use paid work trials in place of conventional coding challenges. Rather than solving isolated interview problems, candidates demonstrate how they approach real engineering work.

Like every hiring format, paid work trials have tradeoffs. They require a greater investment of time from both the company and the candidate, and not every organization is able to structure them effectively. When they are thoughtfully designed and fairly compensated, however, they can provide a more representative picture of day-to-day engineering than a single interview session.
