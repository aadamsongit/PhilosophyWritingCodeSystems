## T-Shaped Engineers

You may have encountered advice like:

"You're not a React developer or a Svelte developer or a Rust developer or a C# developer. You're a developer."

The underlying idea is that durable engineering skills transfer across languages, frameworks, and technology stacks. Strong problem decomposition, debugging, system design, communication, and engineering judgment remain valuable regardless of the tools you're using.

At the same time, you'll also hear advice to become a T-shaped engineer. These ideas aren't in conflict.

The horizontal bar of the "T" represents breadth: familiarity with a wide range of technologies, concepts, and adjacent disciplines. The vertical bar represents depth: developing genuine expertise in a particular area.

Most engineering careers require both.

Breadth helps you understand systems, collaborate across teams, and adapt as technology changes. Depth gives you the ability to solve difficult problems, mentor others, and become someone people trust in a particular domain. You don't have to choose between being "a developer" and developing expertise. Durable engineering careers are often built by combining transferable fundamentals with meaningful depth in one or more areas. Frameworks will change. Your specialty may even change over time. Your ability to learn, adapt, and build deep understanding is what allows both the breadth and the depth to grow.

## Strong Technical Professionals Often Align with Particular Domains

Over time, many engineers develop a recognizable area of depth.

The shape of your "T" may emerge from programming languages, frameworks, architectural patterns, development workflows, tooling, cloud platforms, testing, product domains, or particular areas of technology.

Some engineers become known for frontend architecture. Others for backend systems, developer tooling, infrastructure, security, databases, machine learning, mobile development, or embedded systems. Others develop deep expertise in industries such as healthcare, finance, gaming, manufacturing, or pharmaceuticals. Your specialization is often the result of repeated exposure to similar problems rather than a single deliberate decision.

As your career progresses, people may begin to associate you with certain kinds of work. That's not a limitation. It's evidence that you've developed depth. The important thing is to let your specialization rest on durable engineering fundamentals. Technologies evolve. The ability to learn new tools, recognize patterns, and solve problems within a domain is what allows your expertise to remain valuable over time.

## Domain Experience Is Often an Invisible Filter

One lesson I've learned is that domain experience can quietly influence hiring decisions.

Early in my career, my first professional role was in email development. During that hiring process, I interviewed for two different positions with the same company. I ultimately received an offer for the second role. The first role went to a candidate with more directly relevant domain experience.

That experience taught me that companies aren't always evaluating technical ability in isolation. They're also considering how quickly someone can become effective in a particular business domain.

Healthcare, finance, cybersecurity, gaming, and other industries often have their own terminology, regulations, workflows, customer expectations, and technical constraints. A candidate who already understands those contexts may require less onboarding, making them an attractive choice even when technical ability is otherwise comparable.

Fintech and healthcare, for example, often place significant value on prior domain experience. That doesn't mean it's impossible to enter those industries without it. It does mean that candidates with relevant domain knowledge may have an advantage.

The encouraging part is that domain experience compounds. Every project teaches more than technology. It also teaches you about users, business processes, regulations, and the kinds of problems that define an industry. Over time, that knowledge becomes part of your professional value.
