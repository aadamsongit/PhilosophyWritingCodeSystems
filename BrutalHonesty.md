## Now Let's Talk About Some Frank Truths I Hope Will Help You

From 2023 onward, the software engineering job market has generally been more challenging than it was during much of the preceding decade. Different developers have experienced this shift in different ways, but it has changed the kinds of skills many employers value.

One pattern I have observed is that engineers whose work is centered primarily on implementing straightforward feature tickets have faced greater pressure. As organizations become more selective, they increasingly look for engineers who can reason about tradeoffs, break down ambiguous problems, debug unfamiliar systems, communicate with stakeholders, and understand how technical decisions connect to business outcomes.

Feature development remains an important part of software engineering. The challenge is to grow beyond viewing engineering as a sequence of tickets. As your experience develops, try to understand why a feature exists, how it fits into the larger system, what constraints shaped it, and whether there are simpler or more effective ways to solve the underlying problem.

Looking back, I can see this progression in my own work. While contributing to Dance Chives, I often implemented features from tickets with the help of Cursor. They were interesting problems, and I learned a great deal from them. Over time, however, I became increasingly interested in the layers beneath the feature itself: the architecture, the data model, the testing strategy, the deployment workflow, and the tradeoffs that shaped the implementation.

That shift in perspective has been one of the most valuable changes in my development as an engineer. Owning systems rather than simply implementing features has broadened the kinds of problems I enjoy solving. AI has changed software engineering, but it has not eliminated the need for engineering judgment. If an AI assistant proposes an implementation, your responsibility is to understand it well enough to explain it, defend it, modify it, or reject it.

Organizations are generally more willing to accept AI-assisted development than they are willing to accept unexplained development.
You don't own code because you typed it.
You own it because you understand it.

You don't understand it, you don't own it.
