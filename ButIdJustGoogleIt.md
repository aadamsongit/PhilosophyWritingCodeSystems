## Addressing a Common Complaint: "On a real codebase, I'd just Google it."

There's some truth to that.

Professional software development isn't a closed-book exercise. Engineers consult documentation, search for examples, read source code, ask teammates questions, and increasingly use AI assistants. Knowing how to find reliable information is itself a valuable engineering skill. The challenge is that interviews aren't usually trying to determine whether you can find an answer. They're trying to determine whether you can reason about a problem.

Suppose you search for a SQL query, a React hook, or a Dijkstra implementation. You still have to decide whether the result applies to your situation, understand what it does, adapt it to your codebase, and explain why it's an appropriate solution. The problem with saying, "On a real codebase, I'd just Google it," is that many of your peers would do exactly the same thing. Searching, reading documentation, and using AI assistants are now commonplace. By themselves, they provide little signal about how you think as an engineer.

The same is true of AI-assisted development. Vibe coders can search for answers and generate code on the spot. What distinguishes an engineer is the set of durable mental models they bring to the problem before they touch the codebase.

Those mental models allow you to evaluate search results, question AI-generated code, recognize tradeoffs, and understand how a change fits into the larger system. Tools can accelerate implementation, but they cannot replace an engineer's understanding of the system they're modifying. In the end, the value isn't in knowing where to search. It's in knowing what to do once you've found an answer.
