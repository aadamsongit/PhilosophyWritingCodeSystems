# More Than In-Person Networking

When many people hear the word "networking," they picture conferences, meetups, business cards, or introducing themselves to strangers.

Those are valid forms of networking, but they are far from the only ones.

Modern software engineering is a highly online profession. Some of the strongest professional relationships begin through GitHub contributions, technical blogs, LinkedIn conversations, Discord communities, Reddit discussions, Slack groups, open-source projects, online hackathons, or simply helping someone solve a technical problem.

For many engineers, especially those working remotely, these interactions are more common than traditional in-person networking.

What matters is not where the relationship begins. What matters is that you consistently demonstrate competence, curiosity, professionalism, and a willingness to contribute. Good networking is less about attending the right event than about becoming someone people enjoy working with and remember for the right reasons. Technology has expanded the places where professional relationships can begin. It hasn't changed the qualities that make those relationships valuable.

My own career has reinforced this lesson. I taught English online from 2016 into 2021, and aside from one hybrid role, nearly all of my professional work since then has been fully remote.

That meant I wasn't building relationships by seeing coworkers in an office every day or attending frequent local events. Instead, many of my professional connections developed through remote collaboration, online communities, recruiters, technical discussions, and delivering good work over time.

Not everyone has equal access to traditional networking. Conferences, conventions, and professional events often require travel, registration fees, and flexible schedules. Fortunately, they are only one way to build a professional network. I have attended local developer meetups when I could. They were valuable not only for meeting other developers but also for practicing how to explain my projects, answer questions, and communicate technical decisions. A hiring manager may or may not be in the room, but those conversations are useful regardless. The ability to clearly describe what you've built is a professional skill in its own right.

The most valuable relationships are often built by consistently contributing wherever you already are.
